% \subsection{Jupiter In Full View}

% The gas giants are the museums of the Solar system.  They harbor information regarding the infancy of the Solar system, and the dynamical processes that shaped planetary formation.  Jupiter, the largest of the giants, is of natural interest due to its critical role in creating the current architecture of the outer Solar System.  However, unlike a conventional museum, which may have more incentive to showcase its exhibits, Jupiter veils its bulk properties under powerful winds, planet-sized whirls, and awesome displays of unhindered fluid dynamics.  Thus, it provides a natural laboratory where fluid processes can occur over scales inaccessible on Earth.

% In contrast to Earth, Jupiter has no topography.  Large scale terrestrial storms may draw their power from the oceans and deliver them on to land as they evolve, whereas on Jupiter, storms must be generated in deeper regions and can be sustained for much longer as the planet's atmosphere and interior host a smoother transition.  Thus, for all the myriad of features one can see on Jupiter's cloudy visage, understanding their origins must entail an approach where deeper convective and diffusive processes are treated as active participants in the final picture of the Jovian atmosphere.
